Tuning the Emission Wavelength of Bioluminescence via Peptide-fluorophore conjugates for Multicomponent Imaging

Bioluminescent imaging techniques are uniquely appropriate for analyses of biological function, which demand probes that can report on processes in living organisms over time, as they allow sensitive imaging in cells and tissues. Unlike other imaging techniques, such as fluorescence imaging, bioluminescence does not require excitation light. Bioluminescence occurs when a luciferase enzyme catalyzes the oxidation of the luciferin substrate, resulting in the visible production of light. However, the blue color of NanoLuc, the newest commercially available luciferase enzyme, is tissue impermeable. Modulation of the color is necessary to apply these probes to visualize cell populations in the bodies of live mice. Moreover, bioluminescence imaging in vivo has the limitation of imaging only one single population of cells at a time. With at least two new colors, we are developing the bioluminescence tools to enable visualizing multiple cell populations in mouse models.

Large BiT (LgBiT) and Small BiT (SmBiT) are two subunits of NanoBiT, which is a reporter system based on the luciferase enzyme NanoLuc. We take advantage of bioluminescence resonance energy transfer (BRET) to red shift the color of NanoBit by labeling SmBiT with a fluorophore. When SmBit and LgBit associate, bioluminescence energy is transferred to the fluorophore, re-emitting a longer wavelength of light.
